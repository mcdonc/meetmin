# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 18, 2026
**Duration:** 9 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Rock and drugs. It's an uncomfortable topic but you can't honestly discuss 70s rock without it. Substances shaped the music, the culture, and in many cases, they destroyed the people who made it.

**Sheila:** I think the first thing to acknowledge is that the relationship isn't simple. Some of the greatest music of the era was made by people who were using drugs. Some of the worst tragedies of the era were caused by drugs. Both things are true simultaneously.

**Rick:** The early 70s was a psychedelic hangover. LSD had fueled a lot of the 60s experimentation, and that carried into the 70s — early Pink Floyd, early Genesis, Grateful Dead. Acid expanded perceptions and that genuinely influenced the music. *Dark Side of the Moon* doesn't exist without psychedelic culture.

**Dana:** But by the mid-70s, the drugs changed. Cocaine became the drug of the music industry. It was everywhere — studios, backstages, label offices. And cocaine is a different thing than psychedelics. It doesn't expand your consciousness. It inflates your ego and destroys your judgment.

**Marcus:** Fleetwood Mac is the case study. The band has been open about the cocaine consumption during *Rumours*. Stevie Nicks has said she spent millions on it. Mick Fleetwood has talked about how it fueled the sessions — they could record all night, every night. But the toll was enormous.

**Sheila:** And heroin. Keith Richards was addicted to heroin for much of the 70s. He's talked about it extensively. The Stones' output in the late 70s — *Black and Blue*, *Some Girls* — is uneven, partly because Richards was often barely functional. He survived through some combination of luck and constitution, but plenty of others didn't.

**Rick:** Jimmy Page had a serious heroin problem by the late 70s too. *In Through the Out Door* is largely a John Paul Jones album because Page was checked out. And Bonham's alcoholism — the man died from drinking forty measures of vodka in twenty-four hours. He was thirty-two.

**Dana:** The music industry enabled all of it. Labels didn't intervene because the records were selling. Managers looked the other way. There was no concept of duty of care. These were young people, often from working-class backgrounds, suddenly given unlimited money and access to anything they wanted, with nobody around to say no.

**Marcus:** And the mythology made it worse. The idea that drugs were part of the creative process, that you needed to be altered to make great art — that was actively harmful. It gave people permission to destroy themselves and called it artistic dedication.

**Sheila:** The saddest cases are the ones who didn't survive to tell their story. Jimi Hendrix, Janis Joplin, Jim Morrison, Keith Moon, Bon Scott — these weren't just "rock casualties." They were people. They had families. The romanticization of their deaths is grotesque when you think about it.

**Rick:** But here's the complicated part — do we separate the art from the circumstances? *Exile on Main St.* was recorded in a haze of heroin and cocaine in a damp basement in the south of France. It's a masterpiece. Would it have sounded the same if the Stones were sober and well-rested? Probably not. But does that justify the cost?

**Dana:** I don't think it does. And I think framing it as a trade-off is dangerous. Plenty of great 70s music was made by people who weren't drug-addled. Rush, Steely Dan, the Eagles — those bands were professional and disciplined. You don't need to be high to be creative.

**Sheila:** Steely Dan is interesting because Becker and Fagen were perfectionists who demanded total control in the studio. *Aja* is one of the most meticulously crafted albums of the decade and it doesn't sound like it was made in a haze. It sounds like two obsessive craftsmen working with the best session players in the world.

**Marcus:** The drug culture also intersected with the touring culture. Life on the road was grueling — months away from home, playing every night, traveling in cramped conditions. Drugs were a coping mechanism as much as a creative tool.

**Rick:** And the groupie culture, the excess, the isolation — it all fed into each other. Read any tour memoir from the 70s and it's a cycle of performance, substances, chaos, sleep, repeat. It's no wonder so many people came out damaged.

**Sheila:** The punk movement was partly a reaction to this too. The original punks rejected the decadence of rock star culture — though they quickly developed their own drug problems. Sid Vicious died of a heroin overdose at twenty-one. The cycle just repeated with different aesthetics.

**Dana:** I think the lesson, if there is one, is that the music industry has historically been terrible at taking care of its people. The 70s is when that failure was most visible, but it continued for decades. Only recently have we started to talk about mental health and addiction in music with any seriousness.
