# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 17, 2026
**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Guilty pleasures. The 70s rock songs and albums you love that you'd never put on a "best of" list. No judgment today. Rick, you said you'd have to admit things.

**Rick:** Fine. "Don't Stop Believin'" by Journey. I know, I know. It's 1981, technically, but Steve Perry was doing his thing through the late 70s and I cannot resist that voice. *Infinity* from '78 is a great album and I don't care who knows it.

**Sheila:** Rick! I'm proud of you. Mine is Boston's debut album. *Boston* from 1976 — "More Than a Feeling," "Peace of Mind," "Foreplay/Long Time." It's corporate rock, it's overproduced, Tom Scholz was an MIT engineer who built his own studio — and every single song is a perfect piece of arena rock machinery. I love it unironically.

**Dana:** You know what? I'll respect that. Boston is a guilty pleasure for a lot of people, but the craftsmanship is real. Scholz was obsessive — he spent five years on that debut. The guitar harmonies are gorgeous.

**Marcus:** Dana, your turn. What's your guilty pleasure?

**Dana:** This is hard for me because my whole identity is built on defending unfashionable music. But okay — Electric Light Orchestra. *A New World Record*, *Out of the Blue* — Jeff Lynne basically imagined what the Beatles would sound like with a full orchestra and a disco beat. "Mr. Blue Sky" is the most joyous three minutes in 70s rock. It's ridiculous and I love every second.

**Rick:** ELO! Dana, I didn't expect that from you. I figured you'd say something like "my guilty pleasure is a rare Japanese prog album that only three hundred people own."

**Dana:** I contain multitudes.

**Sheila:** What about you, Marcus?

**Marcus:** Meat Loaf. *Bat Out of Hell*. 1977. Jim Steinman's songwriting is absurdly operatic and overblown — "Paradise by the Dashboard Light" is an eight-minute rock musical about a teenage hookup with a baseball play-by-play metaphor. It's preposterous. And it's glorious. Meat Loaf commits to every note with one hundred percent conviction.

**Rick:** *Bat Out of Hell* has sold forty-three million copies. That's not really a guilty pleasure — that's just a massively popular album.

**Marcus:** But nobody puts it on a "greatest albums" list. It's too campy, too theatrical. Critics dismissed it. And yet people keep listening to it fifty years later. That counts for something.

**Sheila:** Let's do some guilty pleasure songs. Individual tracks. I'll start — "Hooked on a Feeling" by Blue Swede. The "ooga-chaka" version from 1974. It's so dumb. It's so catchy. I cannot skip it when it comes on.

**Rick:** "We Built This City" — wait, that's the 80s. Okay, "Escape (The Pina Colada Song)" by Rupert Holmes. 1979. The lyrics are about a couple who both try to cheat on each other and accidentally find each other through personal ads. It's morally bankrupt and melodically irresistible.

**Dana:** "Carry On Wayward Son" by Kansas. It's prog-adjacent but in the most radio-friendly, fist-pumping way possible. Kerry Livgren wrote a genuinely great song and the vocal harmonies are stunning. I'd never claim it's sophisticated music, but it works.

**Marcus:** What about "Bohemian Rhapsody"? Is that a guilty pleasure or a legitimate masterpiece?

**Sheila:** Legitimate masterpiece. No guilt required.

**Rick:** Agreed. "Bohemian Rhapsody" transcends guilty pleasure status. It's just good.

**Dana:** The guilty pleasure test is whether you'd play it for a music critic. I'd play "Bohemian Rhapsody" for anyone. I would not play "Mr. Blue Sky" for Robert Christgau.

**Marcus:** Ha! Fair test. Alright, tomorrow let's talk about the relationship between 70s rock and drugs. It's impossible to discuss the era without addressing it.

**Marcus:** Action items. Rick, you defended Journey and Steve Perry. Can you put together an honest critical reassessment of three "guilty pleasure" bands from the late 70s — Journey, Boston, and Kansas — arguing for their musical merits without irony? Start July 18th, deliver by July 26th.

**Rick:** See you all tomorrow. And yes, I'll defend those bands proudly. The 26th.

**Marcus:** Dana, you admitted your love for ELO. Can you write up an analysis of Jeff Lynne's production techniques — how he achieved that layered, orchestral sound — and how it influenced later producers? Start July 19th, deliver by July 27th.

**Dana:** Tomorrow. And I'll happily dive into Lynne's production. He's a genius. The 27th.

**Marcus:** Sheila, you brought up "Hooked on a Feeling" and the power of pure catchiness. Can you curate a playlist of ten 70s tracks that are critically dismissed but commercially massive, and make the case that commercial appeal is itself a form of artistic achievement? Start July 18th, deliver by July 25th.

**Sheila:** Heavy topic. But necessary. And that playlist will be fun to assemble. The 25th.
