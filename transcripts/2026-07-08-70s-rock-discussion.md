# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 8, 2026
**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Rhythm sections today. The engine room of 70s rock. Rick, go ahead and get Bonham out of your system.

**Rick:** Gladly. John Bonham is the greatest rock drummer who ever lived. I'll say it plainly. The power, the groove, the feel — listen to "When the Levee Breaks." That drum sound has been sampled more than any other in music history. He recorded it in a stairwell at Headley Grange and it sounds like a god pounding on the earth.

**Dana:** I won't even argue that. Bonham is the standard. But I want to talk about Bill Bruford. He played with Yes and then King Crimson, and his approach was completely different — jazzy, unpredictable, technically brilliant. He never played the same fill twice.

**Sheila:** And Neil Peart from Rush. He joined in '74 and immediately changed what was possible for rock drumming. *2112* and *A Farewell to Kings* — his playing is like a one-man orchestra. The time signatures, the precision, the sheer complexity.

**Marcus:** What about Keith Moon? I know he died in '78, but through most of the 70s he was still doing extraordinary work. Moon didn't play drums like anyone else — he led the band from behind the kit. The Who's sound was built around his chaos.

**Rick:** Moon is the anti-Bonham in a way. Bonham was this massive groove player — powerful but always in the pocket. Moon was all over the place, crashing and thrashing. Both approaches are valid, both are genius.

**Dana:** Let's not forget the bass players. Chris Squire from Yes — he essentially invented lead bass guitar in rock. His Rickenbacker tone on *Close to the Edge* is unmistakable. He played bass like a rhythm guitarist.

**Sheila:** John Entwistle from the Who was doing similar things even earlier. "My Generation" has that incredible bass solo, and throughout the 70s he was this anchor of controlled fury. They called him "The Ox" because he stood perfectly still while Moon and Townshend were destroying the stage around him.

**Marcus:** Geddy Lee from Rush is another one. He's playing bass, singing, and playing keyboards simultaneously. The coordination alone is superhuman. And his bass tone on *Moving Pictures* — although that's technically 1981.

**Rick:** Let's talk about Geezer Butler. Black Sabbath's bass lines are as iconic as Iommi's riffs. "N.I.B.," "Iron Man," "War Pigs" — Butler wasn't just following the guitar. He was writing countermelodies that gave those songs their darkness and depth.

**Dana:** And John Paul Jones from Zeppelin. He's possibly the most versatile musician of the decade. Bass, keyboards, mandolin, arranger — he did everything. "No Quarter" is basically his showcase, and it's one of Zeppelin's best songs.

**Sheila:** I want to mention Tina Weymouth from Talking Heads. She came in at the end of the decade, but her bass playing on *77* and *More Songs About Buildings and Food* is so distinctive. Minimalist, funky, melodic — she proved you don't need to play a million notes to be a great bass player.

**Marcus:** Great mention. And Phil Lynott from Thin Lizzy — bass player, singer, frontman. "The Boys Are Back in Town" has that driving bass line that anchors the whole song. He was one of the most charismatic performers in 70s rock.

**Rick:** The rhythm sections of the 70s were something special. These days, bass and drums are often programmed or at least heavily quantized. Back then, the human feel was the whole point.

**Dana:** The imperfections were what made it breathe. Bonham's slight push and pull against the beat — you can't program that.

**Marcus:** Amen to that. Tomorrow — the vocalists. The voices that defined the decade.

**Sheila:** Robert Plant, Freddie Mercury, Stevie Nicks — where do you even begin?

**Rick:** Exactly the problem. See you all tomorrow.
