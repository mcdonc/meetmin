# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 12, 2026
**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Post-punk and new wave — the tail end of the 70s. Punk opened the door, and then all these bands walked through it and went in wildly different directions.

**Sheila:** This is my favorite era. 1978, 1979 — the music coming out of the UK especially was just extraordinary. Joy Division, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Magazine, Gang of Four, Wire's second and third albums, Public Image Ltd. — it's an embarrassment of riches.

**Dana:** And a lot of it was genuinely experimental. These bands had the punk ethos — anyone can do it, reject the mainstream — but they were pulling in influences from Krautrock, dub reggae, electronic music, funk. The palette was enormous.

**Rick:** I'll admit, this is where I start to come around. Joy Division — *Unknown Pleasures* is 1979, and it's one of the most atmospheric records ever made. Bernard Sumner's guitar doesn't sound like a guitar. Peter Hook's bass carries the melody. And Ian Curtis's voice is haunted.

**Marcus:** Martin Hannett's production on that record is a revelation. He turned a punk band from Manchester into something that sounds like it was recorded in a cathedral on the moon. The space, the echo, the darkness — it's as much about what you don't hear as what you do.

**Sheila:** And Curtis's lyrics. "She's Lost Control," "Disorder," "New Dawn Fades" — he was writing about alienation, mental illness, epilepsy, in ways that rock hadn't touched. It's deeply personal and deeply universal at the same time.

**Dana:** Siouxsie and the Banshees are another band that evolved incredibly fast. *The Scream* in '78 is angular and aggressive. By *Join Hands* and then *Kaleidoscope*, they're incorporating psychedelia, Middle Eastern scales, orchestral arrangements. Siouxsie was fearless.

**Rick:** And John McKay and then John McGeoch on guitar — McGeoch especially. His playing on Magazine's *Real Life* and then later with the Banshees is inventive and textured. He used the guitar as a sound design tool.

**Marcus:** Magazine doesn't get talked about enough. Howard Devoto left the Buzzcocks after one EP and formed Magazine, and *Real Life* is a stunning debut. "Shot by Both Sides" is one of the great singles of the era.

**Sheila:** Gang of Four — *Entertainment!* is right at the end of '79. Andy Gill's guitar playing is all jagged angles and funk. They were as influenced by Marxist theory as by rock music. The album is a critique of capitalism delivered through razor-sharp post-punk. It's brilliant.

**Dana:** And Talking Heads were doing their own thing in New York. *Fear of Music* is 1979 — David Byrne's paranoid, neurotic songwriting, Brian Eno's production, Tina Weymouth's bass, Jerry Harrison's keyboards. "Life During Wartime" is post-punk perfection.

**Rick:** The Eno connection is important. Eno produced Talking Heads, Devo, worked with Bowie on the Berlin trilogy — *Low*, *Heroes*, *Lodger*. He's the thread connecting all these disparate post-punk experiments.

**Marcus:** *Low* by Bowie is 1977 and it's arguably the first post-punk album. Side one is these fractured, electronic-influenced songs. Side two is ambient instrumentals. It horrified Bowie's label. They didn't want to release it. And now it's considered one of his greatest records.

**Sheila:** The end of the 70s is so rich. You've got post-punk in the UK, new wave breaking through — the Cars, Blondie going mainstream, the Police emerging. The seeds of everything that would dominate the 80s were planted in 1978 and 1979.

**Dana:** And some of those seeds were electronic. Gary Numan, Tubeway Army — "Are 'Friends' Electric?" was a number one hit in the UK in '79. A synth-driven song topping the charts — that's a seismic shift.

**Rick:** The 70s started with "Whole Lotta Love" and ended with "Are 'Friends' Electric?" The distance traveled in one decade is staggering.

**Marcus:** Perfect way to frame it. Tomorrow let's talk about the live experience — concerts, festivals, the culture around seeing these bands perform.

**Sheila:** Oh, the stories from those shows are legendary.

**Rick:** I wish I'd been there. Well, for most of it.

**Dana:** See you all tomorrow.
