# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 4, 2026
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Happy Fourth of July, everyone. Today we're tackling prog rock. Dana, you said you have opinions. Floor's yours.

**Dana:** Thank you. So here's my take — prog rock is the most important subgenre of the 70s and simultaneously the most frustrating. At its best, it expanded what rock music could be. At its worst, it disappeared up its own backside.

**Rick:** That's surprisingly diplomatic for you. I expected you to just go full defense mode.

**Dana:** Oh, I will. But let me be fair first. The best prog — early Genesis, Yes at their peak, King Crimson, Pink Floyd — those bands made records that nobody else could have made. *Close to the Edge* by Yes is one of the most technically accomplished rock albums ever.

**Sheila:** See, and that's where you lose me. "Technically accomplished" — when did that become the goal? Rock and roll started as three chords and a backbeat. Prog turned it into a conservatory recital.

**Marcus:** I think there's a middle ground. Pink Floyd is technically prog, but they never lost the emotional core. *Dark Side of the Moon* is progressive in structure — it's a concept album with tape loops and synthesizers — but the songs are about universal human experiences. Madness, death, money, time. It connects.

**Dana:** Exactly. And King Crimson — *In the Court of the Crimson King* came out in '69 but the band kept evolving through the 70s. Robert Fripp is a genius. *Red* from 1974 is heavy, complex, and emotionally devastating. It's not cold or academic at all.

**Rick:** I'll grant you Pink Floyd and King Crimson. But what about Emerson, Lake & Palmer? They did a rock arrangement of "Pictures at an Exhibition." They brought an entire orchestra on tour. Keith Emerson had a piano that rotated in the air. At what point does it stop being rock?

**Dana:** When it stops rocking. And ELP still rocked. "Karn Evil 9" is a banger. "Lucky Man" is a beautiful song. They had excess, sure, but the playing was extraordinary.

**Sheila:** The playing was extraordinary — I'll give them that. But there's a difference between virtuosity that serves the song and virtuosity that serves the ego. A lot of prog falls into the second category.

**Marcus:** What about Genesis? Because I think they're the most interesting case. With Peter Gabriel, they were theatrical and ambitious — *The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway* is this wild, surreal concept album. Then Gabriel leaves, Phil Collins takes over, and they gradually become a pop band. The whole arc of the band mirrors the arc of the decade.

**Dana:** *The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway* is a masterpiece. I'll die on that hill. It's weird, it's sprawling, it doesn't always make sense, but it's visionary. And Gabriel's performances — he'd come out in these bizarre costumes. It was rock as total art.

**Rick:** And then Collins turned "Follow You Follow Me" into a love ballad and the prog fans never forgave him.

**Sheila:** I actually think Collins-era Genesis gets unfairly dismissed. *A Trick of the Tail* is a great album. He's a phenomenal drummer and a genuinely good singer. But yeah, by *Invisible Touch* they were a different band entirely.

**Marcus:** Let's talk about the prog backlash. Punk basically defined itself as anti-prog. Johnny Rotten wore an "I Hate Pink Floyd" t-shirt. Was punk right to rebel against prog?

**Rick:** Punk was necessary. By 1976, rock had become bloated. You had bands touring with semi trucks full of gear, playing thirty-minute songs to stadiums. The Ramones showed up and played fourteen songs in twenty minutes. It was a corrective.

**Dana:** But here's what people forget — the punks loved some prog. John Lydon was a massive Can and Van der Graaf Generator fan. Siouxsie Sioux loved Roxy Music. The actual musicians weren't as anti-prog as the mythology suggests.

**Sheila:** That's a good point. The hatred was more about what prog represented — the rock establishment, the excess, the distance between band and audience — than about the music itself.

**Marcus:** And some of the best late-70s music came from the collision of those worlds. Talking Heads, Wire, Magazine — they had punk energy but prog ambition. Post-punk is basically the synthesis.

**Dana:** Which is why I'll always defend prog. Without it, there's nothing to push back against, and rock just stays in the same three-chord box. Prog expanded the vocabulary, even if some of the sentences were too long.

**Rick:** "Some of the sentences were too long" — that should be prog rock's epitaph.

**Sheila:** Ha! On that note, what's tomorrow's topic?

**Marcus:** Let's do southern rock. A completely different side of the 70s — Lynyrd Skynyrd, Allman Brothers, Marshall Tucker Band. The counterpoint to prog.

**Rick:** Now we're talking. See y'all tomorrow.

**Dana:** I'll try to keep an open mind.

**Sheila:** Sure you will, Dana.
