# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 3, 2026
**Duration:** 9 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Okay, 1971. I said it might be the best year for rock albums ever. Let's make the case. What came out that year?

**Dana:** Where do you even start? *Led Zeppelin IV*, *Who's Next*, *Sticky Fingers*, *Hunky Dory* by Bowie, *Aqualung* by Jethro Tull, *Master of Reality* by Sabbath, the Allman Brothers' *At Fillmore East* —

**Rick:** *L.A. Woman* by The Doors, *Meddle* by Pink Floyd, *Pearl* by Janis Joplin, *Every Picture Tells a Story* by Rod Stewart — and Rod Stewart was actually good back then.

**Sheila:** Ha! He really was. "Maggie May" is a perfect song. And don't forget Joni Mitchell's *Blue*. I know people argue about whether that's rock, but it influenced every songwriter who came after.

**Marcus:** *Blue* absolutely counts in a broad discussion. Carole King's *Tapestry* too — same year. The singer-songwriter movement was as much a part of 70s rock as the heavy stuff.

**Dana:** What gets me about 1971 is the range. You've got the heaviest album ever made at the time in *Master of Reality*, the most intimate album in *Blue*, and everything in between. It's like every possible direction rock could go was being explored simultaneously.

**Rick:** And *At Fillmore East* — that's the greatest live album ever recorded. "Whipping Post" on that record is transcendent. Duane Allman's slide guitar playing is otherworldly.

**Sheila:** Duane died later that year. October '71. He was twenty-four. It's heartbreaking to think about what he would have done with another thirty years.

**Marcus:** Same with the Doors. Morrison died in July '71, just months after *L.A. Woman* came out. That album is this dark, bluesy masterpiece — "Riders on the Storm" feels like a farewell.

**Dana:** The death toll of early 70s rock is staggering when you think about it. Hendrix in '70, Joplin in '70, Morrison in '71, Duane Allman in '71. Four of the most talented people in rock, all gone before twenty-eight.

**Rick:** And yet the music kept coming. That's what's remarkable. The losses were devastating but the decade just kept producing. By the time you get to '73 you've got *Dark Side of the Moon*, *Goodbye Yellow Brick Road*, *Quadrophenia*.

**Sheila:** Can we go back to *Hunky Dory* for a second? I feel like Bowie doesn't always get mentioned in these conversations because he's so associated with glam, but that album is pure songwriting genius. "Changes," "Life on Mars?" — he was operating on a different level.

**Marcus:** Bowie is his own category. He changed personas so often that people sometimes forget how consistently brilliant the music was underneath the costumes.

**Dana:** *Ziggy Stardust* is '72, *Aladdin Sane* is '73, *Diamond Dogs* is '74, *Station to Station* is '76 — he never stopped evolving. Most artists would kill for one of those records. He made them in consecutive years.

**Rick:** That creative pace is something else about the 70s that we've lost. Bands were putting out an album a year, sometimes two. Now artists take three, four, five years between records.

**Sheila:** The label system drove some of that. You were contractually obligated to produce. But there's also something about the era — they didn't overthink it. They played, they recorded, they moved on.

**Marcus:** Good point. Alright, I think we've made a strong case for 1971. Tomorrow let's talk about prog rock — the most ambitious and the most divisive subgenre of the decade.
