# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 16, 2026
**Duration:** 9 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Underrated and forgotten bands. Everyone pick someone who should be more famous than they are.

**Dana:** I'll go first. Gentle Giant. They were a British prog band who made incredibly complex, inventive music through the entire 70s. *Octopus* from 1972 is a masterpiece — medieval music, jazz, hard rock, all woven together with intricate vocal counterpoint. They never had a hit single and they're almost completely unknown outside of prog circles.

**Sheila:** My pick is Big Star. Alex Chilton formed the band in Memphis after the Box Tops. *#1 Record* and *Radio City* are two of the greatest power pop albums ever made. "September Gurls," "Thirteen" — those songs are flawless. But their label botched the distribution and nobody heard the records until years later.

**Rick:** Big Star is such a tragic story. The songs were there, the talent was there. They just got completely screwed by the industry. R.E.M., the Replacements, Teenage Fanclub — they all cited Big Star as a primary influence. Chilton should have been as famous as anyone.

**Marcus:** My pick is Budgie. Welsh heavy metal band, formed in 1967, active through the 70s. They were heavier than Sabbath at times — *Bandolier* from 1975 is crushing. Metallica covered them, Iron Maiden cited them. They should be in every conversation about 70s heavy music and they're almost never mentioned.

**Rick:** Great pick. Mine is Montrose. Sammy Hagar's band before Van Halen poached their sound. The first Montrose album from '73 is a blueprint for 80s hard rock. Ronnie Montrose was a phenomenal guitarist, and Hagar's voice was already incredible. They got overshadowed by Van Halen doing something similar a few years later with more flash.

**Dana:** Can I do a second pick? Caravan. Canterbury scene prog — they were the sister band to Soft Machine. *In the Land of Grey and Pink* is gorgeous — whimsical, melodic, jazzy. They're beloved in prog circles but completely unknown to the general public.

**Sheila:** Fanny. One of the first all-female rock bands to record on a major label. They put out five albums in the early 70s — hard rock, funk, soul. They were signed to Reprise, they had David Bowie championing them. Bowie literally said they were one of the most important bands of the era. And almost nobody remembers them.

**Marcus:** That's a travesty. Why do you think bands like Fanny get written out of history?

**Sheila:** Sexism, plain and simple. The rock press in the 70s didn't take women seriously. When Fanny was reviewed, the reviews focused on what they looked like, not what they sounded like. Same thing happened to the Runaways later in the decade.

**Rick:** The Runaways are another great example. Joan Jett and Lita Ford in the same band — two of the toughest rock guitarists of the era, any gender. *Queens of Noise* is a killer record.

**Dana:** What about Nektar? German-based prog band — *Remember the Future* from 1973 is a two-track concept album that's absolutely mesmerizing. They sold well in the US, actually — their American tour sold out theaters — but they've been forgotten.

**Marcus:** UFO deserves a mention. Michael Schenker joined from the Scorpions and turned them into one of the best hard rock bands of the mid-70s. *Strangers in the Night* is arguably the best live hard rock album ever made. Every guitarist from the NWOBHM scene grew up on that record.

**Rick:** Captain Beyond. They had Rod Evans from the original Deep Purple lineup and Bobby Caldwell — not the singer, the drummer from Johnny Winter's band. Their self-titled debut is psychedelic hard rock that sounds like nothing else. One of those albums where you hear it and think, how did I not know about this?

**Sheila:** The 70s is so deep. You can spend a lifetime digging and keep finding incredible music that didn't get its due. It makes you wonder how many great bands never even got recorded.

**Dana:** That's the heartbreaking thing about any era. The ones we know about are the tip of the iceberg. For every Led Zeppelin there were a hundred bands playing the same circuit who never got the break.

**Marcus:** Sobering thought. Tomorrow let's lighten the mood — guilty pleasures. 70s rock songs and albums that you love but maybe shouldn't.

**Sheila:** That's the point. See you tomorrow.
