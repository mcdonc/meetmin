# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 1, 2026
**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Alright, welcome everyone. So today I wanted to kick things off by talking about what we think defined the sound of 70s rock. What made it distinct from the 60s?

**Dana:** I think the biggest thing was the production quality. By the early 70s you had multitrack recording becoming standard, and bands just had more room to layer things. Think about what Led Zeppelin was doing on *Led Zeppelin IV* — that album sounds massive compared to anything from 1967.

**Rick:** Yeah, but I'd argue it was more about attitude. The 60s had this optimism, this flower-power thing, and by the 70s that had burned off. You got rawer, heavier stuff. Black Sabbath, Deep Purple — that darkness wasn't really there before.

**Sheila:** I don't know if I'd say it wasn't there before. The Doors were pretty dark. But I agree the 70s took it further. There was also a fracturing — you didn't just have "rock." You had prog, glam, punk by the end of the decade, southern rock, arena rock. It splintered.

**Marcus:** That's a great point. The 60s felt more unified in some ways. The 70s is when rock became this big tent with all these subgenres living under it.

**Dana:** And the guitar playing evolved so much. Hendrix obviously bridged the decades, but then you get guys like Jimmy Page, Ritchie Blackmore, Tony Iommi — they were all pushing the instrument in different directions.

**Rick:** Don't forget the rhythm sections. John Bonham changed what drums could do in rock music. And bass players like John Paul Jones or Geezer Butler were writing actual bass lines, not just following the root notes.

**Sheila:** I think the albums themselves changed too. The 70s is really when the album became the primary format over singles. *Dark Side of the Moon*, *The Wall*, *Quadrophenia* — these were conceived as complete works.

**Marcus:** Right, the concept album really hit its stride. Do you think that was a positive development or did it lead to some self-indulgence?

**Dana:** Both. For every *Dark Side of the Moon* there's a *Tales from Topographic Oceans* — and I say that as someone who likes Yes.

**Rick:** Ha! That's fair. I think the ambition was mostly a good thing though. Even the failures were interesting failures. Nobody was playing it safe.

**Sheila:** And that ambition is what eventually triggered punk, right? By '76, '77, kids were looking at these twenty-minute prog epics and saying, "Enough, give me three chords and the truth."

**Marcus:** Which is its own kind of 70s rock. The decade contains its own backlash. Alright, let's pick this up tomorrow — maybe we can dig into specific albums next time.

**Dana:** Sounds good.

**Rick:** I'll bring my list.

**Sheila:** See you all tomorrow.
