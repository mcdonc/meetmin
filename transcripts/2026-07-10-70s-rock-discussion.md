# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 10, 2026
**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** The business of 70s rock. Behind every legendary album there was a label, a manager, a deal. And a lot of those deals were terrible for the artists.

**Rick:** Where do you even start? Led Zeppelin's manager Peter Grant is a fascinating figure. He was physically intimidating — he'd allegedly threaten promoters who tried to shortchange the band. But he also revolutionized the business model. He got Zeppelin a ninety-ten split on concert revenue, which was unheard of. The band kept almost everything.

**Dana:** And Grant helped them establish Swan Song Records in 1974. That was a big deal — a major artist-owned label. It gave Zeppelin complete creative control. No A&R guy telling them to write shorter songs.

**Sheila:** But not every manager was looking out for the artists. Allen Klein managed the Rolling Stones in the late 60s and then the Beatles — and he was essentially a shark. The Stones spent years trying to get their master recordings back from deals Klein had structured.

**Marcus:** The master recordings issue is huge. In the 70s, almost every major label contract gave the label ownership of the masters. The artist created the music, but the label owned it. That's why bands like Pink Floyd and Zeppelin pushed so hard for independence.

**Dana:** Atlantic Records is an interesting case study. Ahmet Ertegun built that label on R&B and soul, but in the 70s it became the home of Zeppelin, Yes, Genesis, the Stones — some of the biggest rock acts in the world. Ertegun had a genuine ear for music, which set him apart from a lot of label heads.

**Rick:** And then you've got David Geffen starting Asylum Records in '71. He signed the Eagles, Jackson Browne, Joni Mitchell, Tom Waits — basically the entire Laurel Canyon scene. Geffen understood that the California singer-songwriter movement was going to be enormous, and he positioned himself perfectly.

**Sheila:** But the flipside of all that Laurel Canyon success was exploitation. A lot of those singer-songwriters were very young when they signed their deals. They didn't have lawyers, they didn't understand royalty structures. Some of them didn't see fair compensation for decades.

**Marcus:** The touring industry transformed in the 70s too. Bill Graham was the most important concert promoter of the era. He ran the Fillmore in San Francisco and then the Fillmore East in New York. He essentially invented the modern rock concert — the lighting, the sound systems, the whole experience.

**Rick:** And then arena rock took over. By the mid-70s, bands were playing to twenty thousand people in sports arenas. The intimacy of the Fillmore gave way to the spectacle of Madison Square Garden. More money, bigger audiences, but something was lost.

**Dana:** The economics drove it. A band could make more money from one arena show than a week of club dates. And the record labels encouraged it because touring sold albums. It became a machine.

**Sheila:** And the drugs were part of the machine. I don't want to romanticize it, but the label-funded excess — the cocaine, the heroin, the partying — it destroyed people. The labels didn't care as long as the records kept coming.

**Marcus:** The classic example is probably Fleetwood Mac's *Rumours*. Warner Bros. was funding a recording process where the band was consuming enormous quantities of cocaine while going through multiple breakups. The label's attitude was essentially: whatever it takes, just deliver the album.

**Rick:** And it worked — *Rumours* sold forty million copies. But at what human cost? Stevie Nicks has talked about how the cocaine nearly killed her. Mick Fleetwood went bankrupt from drug spending.

**Dana:** There's also the payola angle. Radio play was everything, and labels were paying DJs and program directors to play their records. It was technically illegal after the 50s scandals, but it continued under the table through the 70s. If your record didn't get played on FM radio, it didn't exist.

**Sheila:** And FM radio in the 70s was a golden age in itself. DJs had real autonomy — they could play deep album cuts, not just singles. That's how bands like Pink Floyd and Yes built their audiences. Album-oriented rock radio was the discovery engine.

**Marcus:** Until consultants came in and tightened playlists in the late 70s. By 1980, FM radio was becoming the formatted, corporate thing it is today. Another casualty of the business taking over.

**Rick:** The 70s rock business is a story of creativity versus commerce. Sometimes they aligned beautifully. Often they didn't.

**Marcus:** Well put. Tomorrow let's get into punk — the movement that tried to blow the whole thing up.
