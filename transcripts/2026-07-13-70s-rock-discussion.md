# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 13, 2026
**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** The live experience. Concerts and festivals in the 70s. What was it like to see these bands?

**Rick:** I mean, none of us were there — we're all too young — but from the live albums and the footage, it's clear that 70s concerts were a different animal. No phones, no barriers between the crowd and the stage in a lot of cases. Raw, communal, sometimes dangerous.

**Sheila:** The festivals tell the story. After Woodstock in '69, there was this explosion of outdoor festivals in the early 70s. The Isle of Wight Festival in 1970 had over six hundred thousand people. Hendrix, The Who, the Doors, Free, Jethro Tull — all on one bill. It was chaos.

**Dana:** And then there were the disasters. Altamont in '69 cast a shadow over the whole festival concept. By the mid-70s, the big outdoor festivals had mostly died out. The concert experience moved indoors — to arenas and theaters.

**Marcus:** The theater shows are fascinating. Genesis with Peter Gabriel — those were essentially rock operas. Gabriel would come out in elaborate costumes, tell stories between songs, create these theatrical set pieces. The show for *The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway* was a full multimedia experience.

**Dana:** Pink Floyd took it even further. The *Dark Side of the Moon* tour had a circular screen, quadraphonic sound, a crashing airplane. By *The Wall* in '80 and '81, they were literally building a wall across the stage during the show. Roger Waters conceived it as total immersive theater.

**Rick:** And on the other end of the spectrum, you had the stripped-down power of a band like the Who or Led Zeppelin just owning a room with volume and energy. Zeppelin's shows were three-plus hours. Page would play the guitar with a violin bow. Bonham's drum solo in "Moby Dick" could go on for thirty minutes.

**Sheila:** The intimacy of the club shows is what I wish I could have experienced. CBGB — that room held maybe three hundred people. Imagine seeing the Ramones or Television or Blondie in a space that small. The energy must have been incredible.

**Marcus:** And the Roxy in London for punk. It was only open for about a hundred days in early '77 but the Clash, the Damned, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Wire, the Buzzcocks — they all played there. A tiny basement club that changed music.

**Rick:** The sound quality at 70s concerts was often terrible by modern standards. PA systems were primitive compared to what we have now. But that rawness was part of the experience. You weren't hearing a polished reproduction — you were hearing the actual amplifiers, the actual drums, the actual room.

**Dana:** The bootleg culture grew out of that. Fans would sneak tape recorders into shows, and those recordings would circulate on cassette. Grateful Dead encouraged it, which is why their live archive is so vast. But even bands that didn't approve — Zeppelin, the Who — have incredible bootleg recordings out there.

**Sheila:** Free concerts in parks were a big thing too. In the UK especially — bands would just set up and play. Before health and safety regulations clamped down, you could have thousands of people gathered in Hyde Park to see the Stones or Blind Faith. No tickets, no barriers.

**Marcus:** The Stones' Hyde Park concert in '69 — right after Brian Jones died — that's iconic. Mick Jagger released butterflies as a tribute. Two hundred fifty thousand people. It feels almost mythical now.

**Rick:** And Knebworth. Zeppelin played Knebworth in '79 — their last UK shows. Over two hundred thousand people across two nights. By that point, the stadium experience had become the dominant format. The intimacy was gone, but the spectacle was something else.

**Dana:** The transition from clubs to theaters to arenas to stadiums — that's the story of 70s live music in miniature. Each step gained something and lost something.

**Marcus:** Well said. Tomorrow, let's talk about the role of the recording studio — the producers and engineers who shaped the sound of the decade.

**Sheila:** George Martin, Glyn Johns, Eddie Kramer — the invisible architects.

**Rick:** Not invisible to us. See you tomorrow.

**Dana:** Looking forward to it.
