# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 2, 2026
**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** So Rick, you said you were bringing a list. Let's hear it. Top albums of the 70s.

**Rick:** Okay, I'll start. Number one for me is *Led Zeppelin IV*. No question. That album has "Stairway to Heaven," "Black Dog," "Rock and Roll," "When the Levee Breaks" — it's front to back perfect.

**Dana:** I knew you'd say that. It's a great record but I'd put *Dark Side of the Moon* above it. The sonic innovation alone — Roger Waters and the band created something that transcended rock. It was art.

**Sheila:** I'm going with *Rumours* by Fleetwood Mac. I know some people think it's too pop, but the songwriting on that album is untouchable. And the fact that they made it while the band was literally falling apart — that tension is in every track.

**Marcus:** Those are all solid picks. Mine might be controversial — I'd say *Who's Next* by The Who. It came out in '71 and it still sounds modern. Pete Townshend was using synthesizers in ways nobody else was. "Baba O'Riley," "Won't Get Fooled Again" — those songs basically invented a new kind of rock.

**Rick:** That's not controversial at all. *Who's Next* is incredible. But what about the Stones? You can't talk about 70s rock without *Exile on Main St.*

**Dana:** *Exile* is a masterpiece but it's also a mess. In the best way. It sounds like it was recorded in a basement in the south of France — which it literally was.

**Sheila:** I love *Exile* but I think *Sticky Fingers* is a tighter record. "Brown Sugar," "Wild Horses," "Can't You Hear Me Knocking" — every track lands.

**Marcus:** What about the heavier side? Nobody's mentioned Sabbath yet.

**Rick:** *Paranoid* is 1970, so it counts. That album invented heavy metal. Full stop. "Iron Man," "War Pigs," "Paranoid" — Iommi's riffs on that record are like nothing that came before.

**Dana:** I'd actually argue *Master of Reality* is the better Sabbath album. It's heavier, it's more refined — "Children of the Grave" and "Into the Void" are just crushing.

**Sheila:** We should talk about the women too. Heart's *Dreamboat Annie* came out in '76. Ann Wilson had one of the best voices in rock, period. And her sister Nancy was an incredible guitarist.

**Marcus:** Absolutely. And Janis Joplin's *Pearl* — even though she didn't live to see it released. "Me and Bobby McGee" is one of the great rock songs.

**Rick:** That's 1971. Tragic story. But what a record.

**Dana:** We could do this all day. Should we narrow the focus tomorrow? Maybe pick a specific year or a specific subgenre?

**Marcus:** Let's do 1971. It might be the single best year for rock albums ever. But first, some action items. Dana, can you compile a side-by-side comparison of the production techniques on *Led Zeppelin IV* versus *Dark Side of the Moon*? Start July 3rd, deliver by July 11th.

**Dana:** Perfect — I've been wanting to dig into that. July 11th it is.

**Marcus:** Sheila, you brought up *Rumours* and the tension within the band. Can you write up a brief on how personal conflict influenced the songwriting on that album — who wrote what and what was happening between them? Start tomorrow, deliver by July 10th.

**Sheila:** On it. I'll have it by the 10th.

**Marcus:** And Rick, since you're the Sabbath defender, put together an argument for why *Paranoid* versus *Master of Reality* is the better album, with specific musical evidence. Start July 3rd, deliver by July 9th.

**Rick:** Gladly. Though I might end up arguing for *Master of Reality* once I dig in.

**Sheila:** Oh, that's a bold claim. But I'm interested. See you all tomorrow.

**Rick:** Definitely. '71 is stacked.
