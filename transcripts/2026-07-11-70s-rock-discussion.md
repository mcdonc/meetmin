# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 11, 2026
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Punk rock. 1976, 1977 — the explosion that changed everything. Sheila, take it away.

**Sheila:** Punk is the most important thing that happened in the 70s. I know that's a big claim in a decade that includes *Dark Side of the Moon*, but hear me out. Punk didn't just create great music — it democratized music. It said: you don't need to be a virtuoso, you don't need a record deal, you don't need permission. Just do it.

**Rick:** I respect punk but I've never loved it musically. The Ramones are fun for about twenty minutes, and then I want to hear a guitar solo.

**Dana:** I'm with Rick on the sonic side, but Sheila's right about the cultural impact. Without punk, you don't get post-punk, new wave, indie rock, alternative — basically the entire landscape of the 80s and 90s.

**Marcus:** Let's trace the lineage. Where does punk start? Because it doesn't appear out of nowhere in '76.

**Sheila:** The roots go back to the late 60s. The Stooges, the MC5, the Velvet Underground — those are the proto-punk bands. Iggy Pop with the Stooges was doing everything punk would do — the aggression, the confrontation, the short fast songs — by 1969.

**Rick:** And the New York Dolls, like we talked about on glam day. They were the bridge. Sloppy, loud, attitude over technique.

**Sheila:** Then you get to CBGB in New York, mid-70s. The Ramones start playing there in '74. Television, Talking Heads, Blondie, Patti Smith — they're all coming up in this tiny club on the Bowery. Each of those bands sounds completely different, but they share a DIY ethos and a rejection of the rock establishment.

**Dana:** I'll admit, Television is an exception to my prog-fan skepticism about punk. *Marquee Moon* is a masterpiece. Tom Verlaine's guitar playing is intricate, ambitious, emotional — it's essentially prog played with punk attitude. That ten-minute title track is as good as anything from the 70s.

**Marcus:** That's an important point. CBGB punk wasn't all three chords. The Ramones were the minimalists, but Television was complex, Talking Heads were arty, Patti Smith was poetic. It was a scene, not a formula.

**Sheila:** And then the UK. The Sex Pistols — whatever you think of them musically, *Never Mind the Bollocks* is a bomb that went off in British culture. Malcolm McLaren was a provocateur and a con man, but the anger in Johnny Rotten's voice was real. "Anarchy in the U.K.," "God Save the Queen" — these songs terrified the establishment.

**Rick:** I think the Pistols are overrated musically and underrated culturally. Steve Jones is actually a great guitar player, but the mythology overshadows the music.

**Sheila:** The Clash are more important to me. *The Clash* in '77 and then *London Calling* in '79 — Joe Strummer and Mick Jones took the punk template and expanded it. Reggae, ska, rockabilly, jazz — they pulled everything in. *London Calling* is a double album, which is the most un-punk thing imaginable, and it's perfect.

**Marcus:** *London Calling* might be the best album of the decade, full stop. It's punk in spirit but it contains multitudes. "Train in Vain" is basically a pop song. "Guns of Brixton" is reggae. "Lost in the Supermarket" is new wave before new wave existed.

**Dana:** I'll concede *London Calling*. That album transcends genre. But what about the Buzzcocks? I think Pete Shelley was one of the best songwriters of the era. "Ever Fallen in Love" is a perfect pop-punk song — catchy, emotional, three minutes, done.

**Rick:** The Buzzcocks are great. And Wire — *Pink Flag* has twenty-one songs in thirty-five minutes. It's punk at its most efficient. Some of those songs are under a minute and they still feel complete.

**Sheila:** And we should mention the Damned. They released the first UK punk single — "New Rose" — before the Pistols. Captain Sensible and Dave Vanian don't get enough credit. And the Damned were funny, which was rare in punk. A lot of punk took itself very seriously. The Damned were having a blast.

**Marcus:** What about the politics of punk? Because it wasn't just aesthetic rebellion. The Rock Against Racism movement in '77 and '78 — the Clash, Steel Pulse, Tom Robinson Band — used punk shows to fight the National Front. Music as direct political action.

**Sheila:** That's crucial context. Britain in the late 70s was economically devastated — high unemployment, strikes, the Winter of Discontent. Punk was a response to real material conditions. When Rotten sang "No future," he meant it. For a lot of working-class kids, there genuinely wasn't one.

**Rick:** And that's what separates punk from just being a musical style. It was a moment. You can play punk music anytime, but the urgency, the necessity — that was specific to 1976, 1977.

**Dana:** I think what I've always struggled with about punk is the anti-intellectualism. The idea that complexity equals pretension. But I'll grant that the energy and the openness — the idea that anyone could start a band — that's a gift to music.

**Marcus:** Great discussion. Tomorrow let's talk about the albums that bridged punk and what came next — the post-punk and new wave records of the late 70s.
