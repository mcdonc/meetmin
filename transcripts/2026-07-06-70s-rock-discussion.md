# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 6, 2026
**Duration:** 9 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Glam rock. Sequins, platform boots, androgyny, and some of the catchiest songs ever written. Who wants to start?

**Dana:** I'll jump in. Glam is underrated as a musical movement because people focus on the visuals. But the songs — Bowie's "Suffragette City," T. Rex's "Get It On," Roxy Music's "Virginia Plain" — these are brilliant pop-rock songs that happen to come wrapped in mascara and tinfoil.

**Sheila:** Marc Bolan is the one who kicked it off, right? T. Rex went from this acoustic folk thing to full-on electric glam almost overnight. *Electric Warrior* in 1971 is where it starts.

**Rick:** Bolan was the first, but Bowie perfected it. *The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars* — that album is a concept piece about an alien rock star, and it works on every level. As a character study, as a collection of songs, as a cultural statement.

**Marcus:** What Bowie understood, maybe better than anyone, was that rock and roll is performance. It's theater. Elvis knew that, Little Richard knew that. Bowie just took it further and made the persona part of the art.

**Dana:** And Mick Ronson doesn't get enough credit. He was Bowie's guitarist on the Ziggy album, and his playing is ferocious. "Moonage Daydream" has one of the greatest guitar solos in rock. Ronson brought the muscle to Bowie's vision.

**Sheila:** Can we talk about Roxy Music? Because I think Bryan Ferry and Brian Eno together were one of the most interesting partnerships in 70s rock. Eno was doing things with synthesizers and tape manipulation that were genuinely avant-garde, and Ferry was this suave crooner. It shouldn't have worked.

**Rick:** But it did. *For Your Pleasure* is an incredible album. "In Every Dream Home a Heartache" — that song starts as this eerie spoken-word piece about an inflatable doll and then explodes into this massive guitar freakout. It's unhinged in the best way.

**Dana:** And when Eno left, Roxy became more polished and romantic — *Avalon* is the end point — but those early records with Eno were genuinely experimental. They were glam and art rock and avant-garde all at once.

**Marcus:** What about the American side of glam? The New York Dolls, Alice Cooper — they were doing something related but rougher, dirtier.

**Rick:** The New York Dolls are the missing link between glam and punk. *New York Dolls* from '73 — that album is sloppy, loud, and completely thrilling. Johnny Thunders could barely play his guitar and it didn't matter. The attitude was everything.

**Sheila:** And Alice Cooper — the original shock rocker. *Billion Dollar Babies* was one of the best-selling albums of 1973. He brought theatrics to rock in a different way than Bowie — less elegant, more Grand Guignol. Guillotines on stage, snakes, fake blood.

**Dana:** There's an interesting class dynamic too. British glam was largely art-school kids — Bowie, Ferry, Eno. American glam was more street-level. The Dolls were from Queens and Staten Island. Alice Cooper was from Detroit originally. Same aesthetic impulse, different origins.

**Marcus:** And glam had this massive influence on what came next. Without glam, you don't get punk — the Dolls directly inspired the Sex Pistols and the Ramones. Without glam, you don't get new wave. Without Bowie and Roxy Music, you don't get Duran Duran or the Cure or virtually any 80s band.

**Rick:** Even heavy metal borrowed from glam. Def Leppard, Motley Crue, Poison — 80s hair metal is basically glam aesthetics bolted onto hard rock riffs.

**Sheila:** That's a lineage those bands probably wouldn't love to hear, but it's true. The eyeliner came from somewhere.

**Marcus:** Ha! Alright, tomorrow let's talk about the guitar heroes of the 70s. Not just the famous ones — let's dig into some underappreciated players too.

**Dana:** Ooh, I have a whole list for that.

**Rick:** Of course you do. See everyone tomorrow.

**Sheila:** Can't wait. Later, all.
