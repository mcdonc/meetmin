# 70s Rock & Roll Discussion Group
**Date:** July 7, 2026
**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Attendees:** Marcus, Dana, Rick, Sheila

---

**Marcus:** Guitar heroes. The 70s might be the golden age of rock guitar. Who are we talking about?

**Rick:** You have to start with Jimmy Page. What he did with Led Zeppelin — the range alone. He could play acoustic fingerpicking on "Bron-Y-Aur Stomp," heavy riffs on "Whole Lotta Love," and that iconic solo on "Stairway to Heaven." He was also a brilliant producer. The sound of those Zeppelin records is as much about his production as his playing.

**Dana:** Page is the obvious pick, but I want to make a case for Robert Fripp. King Crimson's guitarist. His playing on *Red* and *Larks' Tongues in Aspic* is unlike anything else in rock. He approached the guitar almost like a classical composer — precise, mathematical, but somehow still visceral.

**Sheila:** I'm going to throw in a name people forget — Robin Trower. He came out of Procol Harum and then went solo. *Bridge of Sighs* from 1974 is a Hendrix-influenced masterpiece. His tone is incredible — thick, warm, dripping with vibrato.

**Marcus:** Great pick. Trower is massively underrated. What about Ritchie Blackmore? Deep Purple and then Rainbow — the man basically invented neoclassical rock guitar. He was blending Bach and Beethoven with blues before anyone else.

**Rick:** "Highway Star" — that solo is essentially a Bach fugue played through a Marshall stack. Blackmore was classically trained and he brought that discipline to hard rock. He was also a phenomenal live performer. *Made in Japan* captures that.

**Dana:** Can we talk about some of the less flashy players? I think Andy Latimer from Camel deserves mention. His guitar tone on *Moonmadness* is one of the most beautiful sounds in 70s rock. Very melodic, very lyrical.

**Sheila:** And Steve Howe from Yes. He doesn't always get guitar-hero credit because prog players are seen differently, but his acoustic work on "The Clap" and his electric playing on "Siberian Khatru" — the man could do everything. Classical, jazz, country, rock.

**Marcus:** What about the blues-rock side? Peter Green, before he left Fleetwood Mac, was phenomenal. B.B. King himself said Peter Green had the sweetest tone he'd ever heard. And Rory Gallagher — that guy played with more passion than almost anyone.

**Rick:** Rory Gallagher is a fantastic call. *Irish Tour '74* — that live album is incendiary. He played a beaten-up Stratocaster and he wrung every possible sound out of it. If he'd been American instead of Irish, he'd be a household name.

**Dana:** And we shouldn't forget the women. Bonnie Raitt was doing incredible slide guitar work in the 70s. Nancy Wilson of Heart was a remarkable acoustic and electric player. They didn't get the "guitar hero" label because of the era's sexism, but the playing speaks for itself.

**Sheila:** Thank you for saying that. Nancy Wilson's acoustic intro to "Crazy on You" — that's as technically impressive as anything the guys were doing. She just never got the magazine covers.

**Marcus:** Carlos Santana deserves mention too. *Abraxas* is 1970, and what he was doing — blending Latin percussion and melody with rock guitar — nobody else sounded like that. "Black Magic Woman" has one of the most recognizable guitar tones in history.

**Rick:** Completely unique player. He proved you could have a signature sound without being the fastest or the most technical. It's about feel and tone.

**Dana:** That's the real lesson of 70s guitar. It wasn't just about speed or technical ability. Each of these players — Page, Fripp, Trower, Blackmore, Santana — had a voice on the instrument. You could identify them from a single note.

**Marcus:** Well said. Tomorrow let's talk about the rhythm sections — the drummers and bass players who don't always get their due.
